## Chapter 2: Market Structure and Price Action

2.1 Understanding Market Structure

Market structure is the framework institutional traders use to analyze price movement. It consists of:
	â€¢	Swing highs and swing lows
	â€¢	Trend lines and channels
	â€¢	Support and resistance zones
	â€¢	Break of structure (BOS) points

Why structure matters:
When institutions want to enter large positions, they can't do it all at once without moving the market. They use market structure to identify optimal entry points where liquidity exists.

2.2 Identifying Swing Points

Swing High: A price peak where the high is higher than the surrounding candles on both sides. Typically requires at least 3-5 candles confirmation.

Swing Low: A price trough where the low is lower than the surrounding candles on both sides.

How to mark swing points:
	â€¢	Look for at least 2 lower highs on each side for swing high
	â€¢	Look for at least 2 higher lows on each side for swing low
	â€¢	The more candles confirming, the stronger the swing point
	â€¢	These become key reference points for trend analysis

2.3 Break of Structure (BOS)

Break of Structure occurs when price breaks through a previous swing point, signaling potential trend change or continuation.

Bullish BOS: Price breaks above a previous swing high
	â€¢	Signals potential uptrend continuation or reversal
	â€¢	Smart money may be accumulating
	â€¢	Look for volume confirmation

Bearish BOS: Price breaks below a previous swing low
	â€¢	Signals potential downtrend continuation or reversal
	â€¢	Smart money may be distributing
	â€¢	Watch for increased selling volume

False breaks:
Not every BOS is valid. Watch for:
	â€¢	Low volume breaks (likely false)
	â€¢	Immediate reversal after break
	â€¢	Breaking during low liquidity hours
	â€¢	Gaps that get filled quickly

2.4 Liquidity Pools

Liquidity pools are clusters of stop losses and pending orders that smart money targets:

Where liquidity pools form:
	â€¢	Above swing highs (buy stops)
	â€¢	Below swing lows (sell stops)
	â€¢	At round numbers (100, 200, etc.)
	â€¢	Near previous day high/low
	â€¢	At equal highs or equal lows

Smart money strategy:
	â€¢	Sweep liquidity pools to fill large orders
	â€¢	Create stop hunts to shake out weak hands
	â€¢	Use the liquidity to enter opposite direction
	â€¢	This creates the classic "fake-out then breakout" pattern

## Chapter 3: Order Flow and Institutional Footprints

3.1 Reading Order Flow

Order flow is the lifeblood of smart money analysis. It shows who is in control - buyers or sellers.

Key order flow concepts:
	â€¢	Bid vs. Ask: Where orders are sitting
	â€¢	Aggressor side: Who is paying the spread
	â€¢	Volume profile: Distribution of volume at price levels
	â€¢	Delta: Difference between buy and sell volume

3.2 Volume Analysis

Volume tells you the strength behind price moves:

High volume + price up = Institutional buying (bullish)
High volume + price down = Institutional selling (bearish)
Low volume + price up = Weak rally (caution)
Low volume + price down = Weak selloff (may reverse)

Absorption: When large volume occurs but price doesn't move much, institutions are absorbing the other side's orders. This often precedes big moves.

Example:
Price tries to break support with huge selling volume, but price barely moves down. This shows smart money buying the selling pressure (absorption). Often followed by a strong rally.

3.3 Time and Sales Data

Watching real-time trades reveals:
	â€¢	Large block trades (institutional size)
	â€¢	Aggressive buying (market orders on ask)
	â€¢	Aggressive selling (market orders on bid)
	â€¢	Iceberg orders (hidden large orders)

Red flags:
	â€¢	Series of large sells into a rally = distribution
	â€¢	Series of large buys into a decline = accumulation
	â€¢	Sudden spike in trade frequency = algo activity

3.4 Open Interest Analysis (Futures & Options)

Open Interest (OI) shows the number of open contracts:

Rising OI + Rising Price = New longs entering (bullish)
Rising OI + Falling Price = New shorts entering (bearish)
Falling OI + Rising Price = Short covering (temporary)
Falling OI + Falling Price = Long liquidation (temporary)

For options:
	â€¢	High call OI at strikes above price = resistance
	â€¢	High put OI at strikes below price = support
	â€¢	PCR (Put-Call Ratio) analysis for sentiment
	â€¢	Max Pain theory for expiry targeting

	â€¢	Increased volume on the decline
	â€¢	Limited tests (fresh zones are stronger)

	â€¢	1st test: Still strong

## Chapter 7: Order Blocks and Breaker Blocks

7.1 What is an Order Block?

An order block is the last bearish candle before a bullish move (or last bullish candle before a bearish move) where institutions placed their orders.

Bullish Order Block:
	â€¢	Last down candle before strong rally
	â€¢	Represents institutional buy orders
	â€¢	Price often returns to this level for refill
	â€¢	Acts as support on retest

	â€¢	Last up candle before strong decline
	â€¢	Represents institutional sell orders
	â€¢	Price often returns to this level for distribution
	â€¢	Acts as resistance on retest

7.2 How to Identify Order Blocks

Valid order block characteristics:
	â€¢	Preceded by consolidation or pullback
	â€¢	Followed by strong impulsive move (50+ pips/points)
	â€¢	Clean candle without long wicks
	â€¢	On higher timeframe (4H, Daily preferred)
	â€¢	Fresh block (not yet retested)

The order block zone:
Mark the entire candle body (open to close) as the zone. Institutions placed orders throughout this range, not at a single price level.

7.3 Trading Order Blocks

Entry strategy:
	â€¢	Wait for price to return to the order block
	â€¢	Look for rejection at the block

	â€¢	Stop loss beyond the block

	â€¢	Refine entry using lower timeframe (15min, 1H)

	â€¢	Wait for volume spike on the retest

A breaker block is a failed order block that has broken and now acts as the opposite polarity.

Why breakers matter:
	â€¢	Shows institutions changed their bias

Trading breakers:

## Chapter 8: Fair Value Gaps (FVG) and Imbalances

	â€¢	Three consecutive candles
	â€¢	Middle candle creates a gap
	â€¢	Gap occurs when: Candle 1 high < Candle 3 low (bullish FVG)
	â€¢	Or: Candle 1 low > Candle 3 high (bearish FVG)

	â€¢	Institutional market orders creating rapid movement
	â€¢	Lack of liquidity at those price levels
	â€¢	Smart money urgently filling positions
	â€¢	News events or major order execution

	â€¢	Represents unfilled buy orders

	â€¢	Price leaves gap while moving down
	â€¢	Represents unfilled sell orders
	â€¢	Acts as resistance when price returns
	â€¢	Institutions may add more shorts here

	â€¢	Larger gap = stronger imbalance

	â€¢	Gaps with volume confirmation are priority
